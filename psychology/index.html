Why it matters

Attention is the new scarcity.

The average person is trained, all day, by the most addictive products ever made. Your website is competing with all of them — for the same eyes, in the same thumb.

8sec
A widely-cited estimate of the modern attention span — down from ~12 seconds in 2000.
50ms
How fast a visitor forms a first impression of your site. That's 1/20th of a second.
2.5hrs
Average time per day people spend on social feeds — all of it engineered to keep them scrolling.

The science of the scroll

Six levers big tech pulls every second.

None of this is an accident. The feed is one of the most carefully optimised psychological systems ever built — and it runs on a handful of principles that are over a century old.

01

Variable rewards

Unpredictable payoffs are far more compulsive than reliable ones — it's why slot machines work. Pull-to-refresh is a slot machine: you never know if the next pull brings something good, so you keep pulling.

B.F. Skinner · operant conditioning
02

Dopamine is anticipation

Dopamine spikes on the expectation of a reward, not the reward itself. The scroll is pure anticipation — the brain is chasing the maybe, not the thing.

Schultz · reward-prediction research
03

The Hook Model

Trigger → action → variable reward → investment, repeated until it's a habit. Every major app is engineered around this exact loop.

Nir Eyal · Hooked
04

No stopping cues

Infinite scroll removes the natural "you're done" signal a page-break used to give. Remove the finish line and people keep going. (Its inventor has publicly said he regrets it.)

Aza Raskin · infinite scroll
05

Behaviour = Motivation × Ability × Prompt

A behaviour only happens when all three line up. The lever big tech leans on hardest is Ability — make the next action so easy it's effortless.

BJ Fogg · Stanford Behavior Model
06

Open loops

Unfinished things nag at us — we remember and crave closure on what's incomplete. "Stories end in 24 hours." "3 people are typing." Curiosity gaps keep you in.

Zeigarnik effect
The reality nobody designs for

People don't read websites. They skim them.

A whole generation has been trained to swipe past anything that doesn't pay off immediately.

That's not a flaw in your customers — it's the water they swim in. After hours a day of feeds tuned to perfection, the brain expects every screen to be instantly rewarding. The moment a website asks for patience, the thumb has already moved on.

Eye-tracking research shows people don't read pages top to bottom — they scan in an F-shaped pattern, grabbing the first words of headings and the left edge, and bailing the instant a block looks like work.

So we build websites the way the feed is built: every section earns the next scroll.

Most sites front-load one good hero and then coast — walls of text, a dead "about" section, a contact form buried at the bottom. By the time the offer arrives, the visitor's gone.

We treat every screen-height like its own post in a feed: a hook, a payoff, and a reason to keep going. Information dense enough to skim, paced so the next swipe is always worth it, with the call to act never more than a thumb away.

Nothing on the page is filler. Every bit of information is as worth your visitor's attention as the last.

Scanning & F-pattern behaviour: Nielsen Norman Group eye-tracking studies (2006, updated 2017).

From feed to funnel

The same mechanics. A different goal.

Social apps use these levers to keep you in the app forever. We use them to move a genuinely interested visitor toward one action — book a call — and then let them go. Here's the translation.

⚡ The business case

An addictive site isn't a vanity metric. It's a growth engine.

When a site holds attention, the numbers move in your favour everywhere at once. People stay longer, scroll further, and reach the offer instead of bouncing — and the search engines that decide who gets seen are watching all of it.

More reach

Time-on-page, scroll depth and low bounce are signals Google rewards. Attention-engineered sites rank higher — so more people find you in the first place.

More conversions

A visitor who actually reaches your offer can say yes. Most sites lose people before that. We don't.

More word of mouth

A site that's genuinely pleasant to move through gets screenshotted, shared and remembered. The pull works on your customers' customers too.

So yes — we build sites that are, in the honest sense of the word, addictive. People are drawn to them, and that pull is exactly what brings more of them in.

Where we draw the line

Persuasion, not manipulation.

We use this science to respect attention, not steal it.

Big tech's goal is to keep you in the app forever — that's where dark patterns come from. Ours is the opposite: get the right person to a clear decision quickly, then let them go live their life. Same psychology, pointed at the door instead of the trap.

No fake scarcity timers. No guilt-trip "no thanks, I hate growth" buttons. No infinite-scroll black holes. Just a fast, honest, genuinely engaging site that makes it easy to say yes when the offer's a fit — and easy to leave when it isn't.

That's the cherry on top: all the pull, none of the sleaze.
